CRISIS RESPONSE AND ETHICAL GUIDELINES

1. Introduction
Crisis response involves providing immediate, ethical support to individuals facing acute emotional or psychological distress. Interventions must prioritize safety, legality, and client autonomy.

2. Types of Crises
- Emotional Crises: Sudden overwhelming emotions (grief, panic, anger)
- Psychological Crises: Acute mental health episodes (psychosis, severe anxiety)
- Situational Crises: Life events causing stress (loss, trauma, disaster)

3. Ethical Principles
- Beneficence: Act to promote client safety and well-being.
- Non-maleficence: Avoid causing additional harm during crisis intervention.
- Confidentiality Limits: Recognize exceptions in cases of imminent risk of harm.
- Professional Competence: Respond within your expertise; refer when necessary.
- Cultural Sensitivity: Consider individual backgrounds in crisis assessment and response.

4. Assessment
- Immediate Risk: Determine risk of self-harm, suicide, or harm to others.
- Mental Status: Assess clarity of thought, emotional state, and orientation.
- Resources: Identify available supports, including family, community, or medical help.

5. Intervention Strategies
- Active Listening: Provide a safe space for emotional expression.
- Safety Planning: Develop concrete steps to reduce risk and ensure immediate protection.
- Grounding Techniques: Help clients regain control through breathing, mindfulness, or simple tasks.
- Referral: Connect clients to appropriate mental health professionals or emergency services.

6. Documentation
- Record details of the crisis, interventions applied, and referrals made.
- Maintain confidentiality and secure storage of crisis records.

7. Follow-Up
- Monitor client progress post-crisis.
- Adjust interventions and provide ongoing support ethically and collaboratively.
